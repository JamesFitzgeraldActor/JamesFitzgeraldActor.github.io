James is an actor, teacher and private coach specializing in Shakespeare and classical styles of acting. He is also a company member of PICT Classic Theatre appearing most recently as Clov in Endgame, as well as Dr. Sloper, The Heiress, Didi, Waiting for Godot, Tieresius Oedipus, Thomasheen Sean Rua Sive, Shylock Merchant of Venice, Dinzie, Sharon’s Grave, Fagin, Oliver Twist, as well as 30 other PICT productions. Other Pittsburgh credits include Watson, Hound of the Baskervilles, as well as The Speckled Band (Kinetric Theater), Charles Ives, Charles Ives, Take Me Home (City Theatre), End of the Affair, All the Names (Quantum Theatre), 1776, Royal Family, Born Yesterday, Midsummer Night’s Dream, Forum, Our Town, Murder on the Orient Express (Pittsburgh Public Theater), Candide (Opera Theatre), Heads (Pittsburgh Playhouse). Regionally James has has appeared multiple times at Marriot’s Lincolnshire Theater, Chicago’s Second City, ETC., The Royal George, Apple Tree, among other Chicago venues including sixteen seasons with Chicago Shakespeare Theater. Other regional credits include Urbanite Theatre (Sarasota FL), Cape May Stage, Milwaukee Rep, the Nebraska Shakespeare Festival, North Carolina Shakespeare Festival, Baltimore Shakespeare Festival, and was a company member of the cast of the off Broadway production of Rose Rage, directed by Edward Hall. He is also the recipient of two Joseph Jefferson Awards (Best Supporting Actor), a Jeff Citation (Best Actor), a Jeff nomination for Best Actor, and an OE Award Nomination (Best Supporting Actor). James is a company member of PICT and Featured Artist in 2014; and in 2022 was awarded PICT's Dagda Award for Artistic Achievement. He was also named Performer of the Year 2016 by the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette.

  • I had always held David Thewlis’ portrayal of this character — in the 2000 film version directed by Conor McPherson — as the best I’ve ever seen, but FitzGerald achieves a level of insouciant resignation which surpasses that performance and begins to approach the rarified landscape of silent film masters such as Buster Keaton. His repetitions, and there are many written into the play, such as the ladder sequences, are never banal. You see the same action performed again and again, but it always looks organic. Like Keaton, like Chaplin, we watch this character with fascination, even though we know, in the classic definition of “The Clown” he will never advance the action. But we hear his steps like heartbeats which may never amount to anything, but infuse the room with life, even if it has no purpose.
    Pittsburgh Quarterly Magazine - Endgame
